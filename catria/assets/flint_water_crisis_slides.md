# Flint Water Crisis and Urban Economics
## Additional Slides

---

## The Flint Water Crisis: An Urban Economics Perspective

### The Geography of Vulnerability

**Pre-crisis conditions shaped who was exposed:**
- Population declined from 200,000 to 100,000 (deindustrialization)
- Majority Black population (~60%)
- ~40% poverty rate
- 1 in 6 homes abandoned

**Key insight**: Where you lived determined your exposure
- Older neighborhoods = older lead pipes = higher contamination
- Low-income residents less able to relocate
- Geographic sorting created differential vulnerability

---

## Neighborhood Effects in the Flint Crisis

### Spatial Inequality in Health Outcomes

**Pre-Switch Geography Mattered:**
- Housing age distribution
- Property values and maintenance
- Infrastructure quality
- Distance from treatment centers

**Post-Switch Neighborhood Variation:**
- Lead levels tripled in some neighborhoods
- ~17% of homes exceeded 15 ppb federal limit
- >40% exceeded 5 ppb "serious problem" threshold
- Blood-lead levels in children doubled citywide, tripled in certain areas

---

## The Economics of Place-Based Exposure

### Why Location Determined Health Outcomes

**1. Housing stock characteristics**
- Older homes → lead service lines
- Lower property values → deferred maintenance
- Concentrated in specific neighborhoods

**2. Immobility constraints**
- Poverty limits residential mobility
- Homeownership as trap during crisis
- Information asymmetries about contamination

**3. Institutional neglect**
- Under-resourced neighborhoods = weaker advocacy
- Environmental racism: majority-Black city under emergency management
- Cost-cutting decisions disproportionately harmed vulnerable populations

---

## Long-Run Neighborhood Effects

### Health Impacts and Future Outcomes

**Immediate crisis (2014-2015):**
- ~9,000 children exposed for 18 months
- 12 deaths from Legionnaires' disease
- 87+ additional Legionnaires' cases

**Long-term neighborhood consequences:**
- Lead exposure → cognitive impairment
- Reduced educational attainment
- Lower lifetime earnings
- Perpetuates neighborhood disadvantage

**The urban economics lesson:** 

*Initial neighborhood sorting + environmental shock → compounded inequality through health channel*

---

## Policy Implications: Breaking the Cycle

### Addressing Place-Based Health Risks

**What the crisis revealed:**
- Infrastructure investment is neighborhood-specific
- Emergency management dissolved local accountability
- Cost-benefit analysis ignored distributional effects

**Urban policy responses:**
- Universal lead pipe replacement (2017 settlement)
- Mandatory water testing and filters
- Bottled water distribution system
- Health monitoring programs

**Broader lessons for urban economics:**
- Public infrastructure as determinant of neighborhood quality
- Residential sorting amplifies initial disadvantages
- Place-based interventions needed for place-based harms

## Connecting to Urban Theory

### Theoretical Frameworks

**Spatial Mismatch Extended:**
- Not just jobs, but environmental quality
- Infrastructure age varies by location
- Historical patterns persist

**Tiebout Sorting Failure:**
- Can't "vote with feet" when:
  - Information is hidden
  - Resources constrain mobility
  - Crisis emerges suddenly

**Neighborhood Effects Channels:**
1. **Physical environment**: Water contamination
2. **Institutional resources**: Weak emergency response
3. **Social networks**: Community organizing succeeded where government failed

**Cumulative causation:**
- Economic decline → cost-cutting
- Cost-cutting → contamination  
- Contamination → health effects
- Health effects → further decline

## Timeline of Events

### From Cost-Cutting to Crisis

**2011**: Flint under state emergency management ($25M deficit)

**April 2014**: Water source switched from Detroit to Flint River
- Inadequate corrosion treatment
- Lead leaches from aging pipes

**2014-2015**: Residents complain; officials claim water is safe

**September 2015**: 
- Virginia Tech study: 17% of homes exceed federal lead limits
- Pediatrician reports doubled blood-lead levels in children

**2016**: Residents sue for safe water; federal judge orders action

**March 2017**: Major settlement requires lead pipe replacement

## Urban Economics Takeaways

### Where You Live = What You're Exposed To

**The Flint case demonstrates:**

1. **Neighborhood quality is multidimensional**
   - Not just schools, crime, amenities
   - Also infrastructure, environmental hazards, institutional capacity

2. **Residential immobility amplifies shocks**
   - Poor residents can't easily relocate
   - Housing wealth trapped during crisis
   - Creates concentrated, persistent disadvantage

3. **Historical patterns matter**
   - Deindustrialization → population decline → fiscal crisis
   - Infrastructure age reflects neighborhood history
   - Past segregation determines present vulnerability

4. **Place-based policies are essential**
   - Universal interventions needed for universal infrastructure
   - Can't rely on individual solutions to collective problems
   - Environmental justice requires geographic targeting
